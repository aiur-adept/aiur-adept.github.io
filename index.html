a short tale
by NEST
The wooden cart turned along the winding road out of the
Pirodel woods and on into the village, carrying Miroda’s mother, her
trunk, several baskets of all sorts (a fair load of firewood, a bearskin,
and an old amphora of landemylk) and of course the driver, Mr.
Ajahn.
DAY 1
“We’ll be arriving soon, miz Irmaondadje, best to wake up!” as
he slapped the wood plank he sat on to tell the horse to perk up its
pace. Brisk cold autumn air had ensured Lynne was already awake, so
she answered – albeit as an awakened sleeper – “Thank you very
much Mr. Ajahn.” Red, gold & brown leaves were dancing in the
misty cool stillness of the far-across wood’s eves as her eyes scanned
the new world with not a little excitement, the anticipation a birth
brings with it. As she drank some sweet riverwater (the rivers were
cleanest in that day) from her gourd, its nourishment awoke the little
seedlin Miroda who turned over in her own sweet dreaming, in her
womb.
“Yes, it feels like rain,” he laughed as the sudden spitting mist
became drips and true raindrops, but yet sparse enough not to cause a
care to arise. Miroda’s mother – Lynne Irmaondadje – stuck out here
tongue to taste the sweetness of the first rain in her freedom, and said
quietly to the soft air, amid the beautiful anonymity of the cartwheels’
turnings and the horsesteps-in-fresh-trail-mud,
“My dear Miroda… you’re finally awake”
… and in her mind, she heard the baby Miroda already born
coo, and then return to sweet dreams of some far-off, somewhere over
the horizon.
NIGHT 1
Lit by moonglow, come nightfall after the sky had shown its
poem of colours, she was welcomed in at last to the home & hearth
the people of Pirodel village had prepared for her after a fruitful
season; the season had begun with her raven’s message of the
desperate flight she would undertake for her daughter’s equal
freedom. Inside, the hearthfire lazed as the women – three – set about
their finishing adjustments. Miroda’s raven & that of her mother had
returned from their day’s ranging & gathering with a hazelnut each,
and their tiny shadows danced on the wall just as the four women’s
did, Lynne at last safe in this wonderful home.
“Well, there we are, miz Irma, your home is among ours.”
“Thank you ladies… I…”
… tears soon came to all their eyes, and they each embraced
each other, then at last under the love of the reflecting moon, they
slept, and well.
DAY 2
Technicolor dreamer
The text scrolling on the terminal was orange dot-matrix;
The technician sitting at the terminal awaited asynchronously –
that is, they wore no watch as they sat eyes-closed awaiting the train
to pull up. The orange light-text was scrolling endlessly: “3:10 YM…
3:15 PX… 3:33 TR…” and on it looped as though in truth a gold-
orange ringband of written sound; but truly the computer was – a
microcontroller embedded in the sign – simply running an old goto
program that looped in space & time. The ring phenomenon was an
extraordinary popular delusion – there was no text.
“Our minds produce the ring, each moment is actually new”
“Ilu alu, avatar, aluvitar, elle u eru evatar… ilu…”
“WAKE UP from discursive thinking!” -- with a snap these
words arrived in the stream. She woke up out of the screen, her black
hair unchanging as the train in the program arrived. The technician
opened her eyes and noticed how her posture had slumped.
“Miroda, your teminal bell’s going off” the slack channel
notification bubble read. She was deaf, but she could read.
“Thx” she quick-replied. Indeed the orange scrolling matrices
were indicating she’d tuned it properly finally, hence the bell – the
divine recursion at the edge of chaos: the nexus, the source, the
ineffable, inexpressible, uncompressable;
“I’ve got to go catch a train guys, will finish from home” she
typed in sacred silence, then closing her machine, folding it, lifting
her bag and heading for the exit, but not too quickly, as she wished to
preserve her meditative peace. “It’s just an exit in the building where I
work…” she reminded herself. Her hair felt the movement through
space and as a sense – in a sense – each strand sang a song of braid,
plait, warp & weft, of the beautiful and irretrievable swirl-moments of
the air, so different to when she’d gone shaven-headed with
expectation of becoming a bikkhuni. She took the stairs, seeing both
elevator banks were busy, and halfway up thought, “I should text her.”

DAY 4
Lynne awakened to sunlight streaming through her window.
Letting down her hair, naked at the basin-bowl, she soaked her hands
and spat chewed mintleaf in to swirl, and read the weather for this
first new day in her new home. The disk of the sky revolved outside
on from dark with twinkling stars toward the haze of blue morning
that hides them. Then, tasting the breaths of first speaking, she said,
“heat from fire, radiance, radiance, radiance, sunrays, moonlight…”
and splashed her face with the awakening-water. Venus was in transit
among the points of the Hunter, and just as the wheel of the cart
turned so many times to arrive again at the same statio, she crossed
the threshold of this new dwelling into the beauty-hued soft dawn
light of morning’s poem, already unfolding. Mrs. Adel was at her
spinning wheel and Mrs. Nowak was taking a bundle of cloth over to
the storehouse; just as “follows the ox” follows “just as the wheel of
the cart”, Mr. Nowak followed happily in her footsteps to bring the
second bundle. Lynne – Miroda’s mother – walked to where Marie
was at the baking area, and, gathering flower-petals from her waist-
satchel, she spoke into them in her palm, “May you bring a surplus of
joy to the eaters; thank you, O Flowers, for the gift of your sacred
flesh…” and Marie smiled, letting her add them in the dough.
